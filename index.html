Why We Buy? From Fast Fashion to Digital Goods

Baumann Elias

Why We Keep What We Keep. Breaking Storytelling Narratives Through the Lens of Consumer Culture.

Consumption culture is no longer the act of buying. It is a way of storytelling, a system that has taught us over many years how to read meaning, identity, and belonging. Each object, a jacket, an old image, a smartphone and even a NFT explains a Story. More often than not, it’s not our own. It is a product of advertising, habit, expectations, and social patterns.
(Baudrillard, 1970)

What I will research in my master’s thesis Breaking Storytelling Narratives can be observed surprisingly clearly in the world of consumption as well. We live in narratives which we’ve never choosen. The question now is: How can we break through them?

To clarify the connection, it’s worth to start with an observation. People collect, buy and own things not only because they need it. People do it because they got told the object has a meaning. Status, certainty, identity, success, belonging and even a certain freedom. Because of that, consumption object are less items and more mini-narratives, which we carry around. We carry them in the backpack, in the wardrobe and in our instagram feed.

But if everything is a narrative, can design break it?



Buying as Storytelling. Why does it work?

Every advertisement is, at its core, a small story. It shows us not only the reality of a product, more of a possible world in which the product has its own role. A t-shirt is not just a t-shirt. It’s a symbol of youth, rebellion, sustainability, style, belonging to a subculture or in case of fast fashion; availability.
(Barthes, 1957), (Joy et al., 2012)

Brands tells us: “This is you if you buy this.”

The world of consumption follows the same mechanisms as classical storytelling.

There is a hero (this is us).
There is a promise (who can we become).
There is a transformation (act of buying)
There is a feeling of finishing (short term)

(McKee, 1997)



Minimalism

Counter-narrative* or just another story?

In the first sight, minimalism looks like the opposite. Buy less, own less, use less. But even minimalism is a narrative. It has its own images, for example empty rooms, perfect folded clothes or the same neutral colors. In addition it has its own rituals like throwing away, reducing and its own promises such as clarity, freedom and focus.

Minimalism as well sells its identity but with less objects.

(Kondo, 2011)

The interesting part in this is: minimalist lifestyles break certain consumption narratives, but at the same time they create new ones. A minimalistic apartment is as well a statement like a high tech-desk. The question, then, is not whether we need narratives, but which ones we choose by our own and which ones steer us without us noticing.

For Breaking Storytelling Narratives it means we can not just turn of narratives. But we can make them visible and even distort them, shift them, recombine them.

Minimalism helps us see how much the “more” of consumption was often just a well-told story. At the same time, it shows us every “less” is framed in a narrative as well.
(Ivanhoe, 2020)



*Counter-narratives are stories that convey the experiences and perspectives of historically oppressed, excluded, or silenced groups, particularly in educational contexts. They serve to validate these perspectives and challenge dominant hegemonic narratives, making them valuable tools in equity courses and teacher education programs.
(„Counter-narratives”, o. J.)



Objects as containers of memory

An important part of my master thesis is, how visual media can evoke reflection in the viewer. In the field of consumption, we can observe this in souvenirs like a t-shirt from Paris or a grandfathers watch.
(Csikszentmihalyi & Rochberg-Halton, 1981)

Objects like this are no longer products. They are frozen moments. Their meaning is not influenced by advertising or consumption structures, it’s only the one life-story.
(Belk, 1988)

This shows me, objects can have narratives, which are more authentic than what the consumption culture is telling us. For my research interest, this is relevant because it proves that narratives can emerge from completely unexpected material.

For my research interest, this is relevant because it proves that narratives can emerge from completely unexpected material. An object can have a story, without having the typical “Story”. My question now is: What happens when storytelling doesn’t explain, but instead leaves space for personal meaning?

That’s one of the central ideas driving my thesis project.



Digital Products

The peak of consumed narratives

NFTs, skins, game items or virtual mods don’t even exist in its physical form. Still people pay money for it. Why? Because digital products are narrative objects only. They only exist as a meaning or a symbol. It’s similar to the brand fashion but in a digital field, where identity is even faster and more flexible. Digital objects shows how quick narratives gets created, as soon people want to see a meaning in it.
(Kerin et al., 2021),(Nakamura, 2020)

NFTs are basically the most extreme way of Storytelling. An image receive a value, because the story about it is got sold. The story is the reason an object receives value, not the object itself. For me, this shows me how fragile narrative structures in the digital field can be and how easy it is to break or reorder them.



Conclusion

When we ask why people collect, buy, and own things, we arrive at a simple truth. We follow stories. Whatever if it’s about sustainable Fashion, minimalism, NFTs or old images, there is always a story behind. Thats why the connection to my thesis is that strong. Storytelling is not just a method of a movie, it’s as well a structure in our daily life.

And if storytelling is everywhere, then design has the task not only to use it, but to research it, irritate it, deconstruct it and recombine it.

An interactive shortfilm, that breaks narratives, is a perfect tool for it. Because it works like our consumer culture. It offers options, evokes expectation, shows alternatives, forces choices and keeps space for own interpretation. This is how everything comes together. Consumer culture tells stories, often without being asked.

Experimental storytelling tells less, but shows more.

And that’s where the possibility for reflection emerges. About ownership, about objects, about technology, about nature, and last but not least about ourselves.

Materialism and Psychological Well-Being

Abstract: This text examines materialism as a psychological value orientation and its association with psychological well-being. Drawing on research from self-determination theory, hedonic adap-tation and social comparison theory, it outlines key mechanisms through which materialistic values are linked to lower life satisfaction and increased psychological distress. The findings suggest that materialism emphasizes external rewards and unstable sources of self-worth, limiting its capacity to support long-term well-being.

Materialism is a central feature of modern consumer societies, where goods are valued not only for their practical use but also for their symbolic meaning in terms of identity and social status. Advertising and social media reinforce this orientation by linking consumption to success and per-sonal value. From a psychological perspective, this raises the question of how a strong focus on material values relates to individual well-being.



Materialism as a Value Orientation

In psychology, materialism is commonly understood as a value orientation in which money, pos-sessions, and consumption are seen as important sources of happiness, success and social recog-nition
(Kasser & Ryan, 1996).

Across many studies, materialism is consistently linked to lower levels of personal well-being. Peo-ple who strongly prioritize wealth, image and external success tend to report less life satisfaction and more psychological strain compared to those who focus on personal growth, relationships and meaningful experiences
(Kasser & Ryan, 1996; Dittmar et al., 2014).

This distinction is often described as the difference between external and internal life goals. Exter-nal goals focus on approval, appearance and social status, while internal goals focus on relation-ships, self-development and emotional connection. Research suggests that a strong focus on ex-ternal goals is less strongly associated with long-term well-being
(Kasser & Ryan, 1996).



Psychological Mechanisms

One important psychological mechanism related to consumption and materialism is hedonic ad-aptation. This concept refers to the tendency for emotional reactions to positive events, including material gains, to be temporary, as individuals often adapt to changes in their circumstances over time. As a result, increases in happiness following consumption tend to diminish, which limits the long-term impact of material goods on subjective well-being
(Diener et al., 2006).

Another important factor in consumption-driven behaviour is social comparison. According to Festinger’s (1954) social comparison theory, individuals evaluate their abilities and opinions by comparing themselves to others, particularly in the absence of objective standards. In contempo-rary, media-saturated environments, idealized representations of lifestyles and consumption pat-terns can intensify such comparisons, which may contribute to feelings of dissatisfaction or inade-quacy. In this context, materialistic consumption may serve as a short-term coping response aimed at restoring a sense of belonging or social status
(Festinger, 1954).

Advertising can further amplify these psychological mechanisms by associating products with emo-tional and symbolic meanings rather than purely functional attributes. Research on judgment and decision-making suggests that many everyday decisions are strongly influenced by intuitive and automatic cognitive processes, rather than by deliberate rational analysis
(Kahneman, 2012).

These insights help explain why advertising appeals that target emotions such as social belonging or fear of missing out can be particularly effective, thereby addressing psychological motives that extend beyond the functional value of consumer goods.



Consequences for Psychological Well-Being

As mentioned, a strong materialistic value orientation has been consistently associated with lower life satisfaction and poorer psychological well-being. Individuals who place high importance on extrinsic goals such as wealth and social status report, on average, higher levels of depressive symptoms, anxiety, and other indicators of psychological distress than those who prioritize intrinsic goals
(Kasser et al., 2014).

One central explanation for this association lies in the instability of externally defined self-worth. Within the framework of Self-Determination Theory, materialism is understood as a value orientation that emphasizes external rewards and approval. A strong focus on possessions, income, and status may interfere with the satisfaction of basic psychological needs, namely autonomy, competence and relatedness, which in turn increases vulnerability to psychological distress
(Dittmar et al., 2014).

Materialistic values may also shift attention away from non-material sources of well-being. Empirical research suggests that higher materialism is associated with lower quality of social relationships, reduced engagement in meaningful activities and lower overall need satisfaction. Over time, this imbalance can contribute to experiences of reduced emotional fulfilment and social connectedness, as personal resources are increasingly directed toward consumption rather than personal development or social bonds
(Kasser et al., 2014).
