Narratives of Consumption. How Objects Construct Meaning, Identity, and Belonging



Baumann Elias

Why We Keep What We Keep. Breaking Storytelling Narratives Through the Lens of Consumer Culture.

Abstract: What I will research in my master’s thesis Breaking Storytelling Narratives can be observed surprisingly clearly in the world of consumption as well. We live in narratives which we’ve never chosen. The question now is: How can we break through them?

To clarify the connection, it’s worth to start with an observation. People collect, buy and own things not only because they need them. They do it because they are told the objects have a meaning. Status, certainty, identity, success, belonging and even a certain freedom. Because of that, consumption objects are less items and more mini-narratives, which we carry around. We carry them in our backpacks, in the wardrobe and in our instagram feed.

But if everything is a narrative, can design break it?



Buying as Storytelling. Why does it work?

Consumption culture is no longer the act of buying. It is a way of storytelling, a system that has taught us over many years how to read meaning, identity, and belonging. Each object, a jacket, an old image, a smartphone and even a NFT* explains a story. More often than not, it’s not our own. It is a product of advertising, habit, expectations, and social patterns.
(Baudrillard, 1970)

Every advertisement is, at its core, a small story. It shows us not only the reality of a product, but also a possible world in which the product has its own role. A t-shirt is not just a t-shirt. It’s a symbol of youth, rebellion, sustainability, style, belonging to a subculture or in case of fast fashion, a symbol of availability.
(Barthes, 1957;Joy et al., 2012)

Brands tell us: “This is you if you buy this.”

The world of consumption follows the same mechanisms as classical storytelling.

There is a hero (this is us).
There is a promise (who can we become).
There is a transformation (act of buying)
There is a feeling of finishing (short term)

(McKee, 1997)





*NFT stands for ‘non-fungible token’. Non-fungible means that something is unique and can’t be replaced. By contrast, physical money and cryptocurrencies are fungible, which means they can be traded or exchanged for one another. Every NFT contains a digital signature which makes each one unique. NFTs are digital assets and can be photos, videos, audio files, or any another digital format. Examples of NFT examples include artwork, comic books, sports collectibles, trading cards, games and more.
(Kaspersky, o. J.)



Minimalism

Counter-narrative* or just another story?

At the first sight, minimalism looks like the opposite. Buy less, own less, use less. But even minimalism is a narrative. It has its own images, for example empty rooms, perfectly folded clothes or the same neutral colors. In addition it has its own rituals like throwing away, reducing and its own promises such as clarity, freedom and focus.

Minimalism as well sells its identity but with less objects.

(Kondo, 2010)

The interesting part in this is: minimalist lifestyles break certain consumption narratives, but at the same time they create new ones. A minimalistic apartment is just as much a statement like a high-tech-desk. The question, then, is not whether we need narratives, but which ones we choose by our own and which ones steer us without us noticing it.

For Breaking Storytelling Narratives it means we can not just turn off narratives. But we can make them visible and even distort them, shift them, and recombine them.

Minimalism helps us to see how much the “more” of consumption is often just a well-told story. At the same time, it shows us every “less” is framed in a narrative as well.
(Ivanhoe, 2020)



*Counter-narratives are stories that convey the experiences and perspectives of historically oppressed, excluded, or silenced groups, particularly in educational contexts. They serve to validate these perspectives and challenge dominant hegemonic narratives, making them valuable tools in equity courses and teacher education programs.
(„Counter-narratives”, o. J.)



Objects as containers of memory

An important part of my master thesis is, how visual media can evoke reflection in the viewer. In the field of consumption, we can observe this in souvenirs like a t-shirt from Paris or a grandfather's watch.
(Csikszentmihalyi & Rochberg-Halton, 1981)

Such objects are no longer products. They are frozen moments. Their meaning is not influenced by advertising or consumption structures, it’s simply the unique life-story.
(Belk, 1988)

This shows me that objects can have narratives, which are more authentic than what the consumption culture tells us. This is relevant to my research interest, because it shows that narratives can emerge from completely unexpected material.

For my research interest, this is relevant because it proves that narratives can emerge from completely unexpected material. An object can have its own story, without following the typical definition of a “Story”. My question now is: What happens when storytelling doesn’t explain, but instead leaves space for personal meaning?

That’s one of the central ideas driving my thesis project.



Digital Products

The peak of consumed narratives

NFTs, skins, game items or virtual mods don’t even exist in a physical form. Still people pay money for them. Why? Because digital products are narrative objects only. They only exist as a meaning or a symbol. It’s similar to the brand fashion but in a digital space, where identity is even faster and more flexible. Digital objects show how quick narratives are created, as soon as people look for meaning in them.
(Kerin et al., 2021; Nakamura, 2020)

NFTs are basically the most extreme way of storytelling. An image receives a value, because the story behind it has been sold. The story is the reason an object receives value, not the object itself. For me, this shows me how fragile narrative structures in the digital space can be and how easily they can be broken or reordered.



Conclusion

When we ask why people collect, buy, and own things, we arrive at a simple truth. We follow stories. Whether if it’s about sustainable fashion, minimalism, NFTs or old images, there is always a story behind them. That is why the connection to my thesis is so strong. Storytelling is not just a method in movie, it’s as well a structure in our daily lives.

And if storytelling is everywhere, then design has the task not only to use it, but to research it, irritate it, challenge it and recombine it.

An interactive shortfilm, that breaks narratives, is a perfect tool for this. Because it works like our consumer culture. It offers options, evokes expectation, shows alternatives, forces choices and leaves space for personal interpretation. This is how everything comes together. Consumer culture tells stories, often without being asked.

Experimental storytelling tells less, but shows more.

And that’s where the possibility for reflection emerges. About ownership, about objects, about technology, about nature, and last but not least about ourselves.

Materialism and Psychological Well-Being

Abstract: This text examines materialism as a psychological value orientation and its association with psychological well-being. Drawing on research from self-determination theory, hedonic adaptation and social comparison theory, it outlines key mechanisms through which materialistic values are linked to lower life satisfaction and increased psychological distress. The findings suggest that materialism emphasizes external rewards and unstable sources of self-worth, limiting its capacity to support long-term well-being.

Materialism is a central feature of modern consumer societies, where goods are valued not only for their practical use but also for their symbolic meaning in terms of identity and social status. Advertising and social media reinforce this orientation by linking consumption to success and per-sonal value. From a psychological perspective, this raises the question of how a strong focus on material values relates to individual well-being.



Materialism as a Value Orientation

In psychology, materialism is commonly understood as a value orientation in which money, possessions, and consumption are seen as important sources of happiness, success and social recognition.
(Kasser & Ryan, 1996)

Across many studies, materialism is consistently linked to lower levels of personal well-being. People who strongly prioritize wealth, image and external success tend to report less life satisfaction and more psychological strain compared to those who focus on personal growth, relationships and meaningful experiences.
(Kasser & Ryan, 1996; Dittmar et al., 2014)

This distinction is often described as the difference between external and internal life goals. Exter-nal goals focus on approval, appearance and social status, while internal goals focus on relationships, self-development and emotional connection. Research suggests that a strong focus on external goals is less strongly associated with long-term well-being.
(Kasser & Ryan, 1996)



Psychological Mechanisms

One important psychological mechanism related to consumption and materialism is hedonic adaptation. This concept refers to the tendency for emotional reactions to positive events, including material gains, to be temporary, as individuals often adapt to changes in their circumstances over time. As a result, increases in happiness following consumption tend to diminish, which limits the long-term impact of material goods on subjective well-being.
(Diener et al., 2006).

Another important factor in consumption-driven behaviour is social comparison. According to Festinger’s (1954) social comparison theory, individuals evaluate their abilities and opinions by comparing themselves to others, particularly in the absence of objective standards. In contemporary, media-saturated environments, idealized representations of lifestyles and consumption patterns can intensify such comparisons, which may contribute to feelings of dissatisfaction or inadequacy. In this context, materialistic consumption may serve as a short-term coping response aimed at restoring a sense of belonging or social status.
(Festinger, 1954)

Advertising can further amplify these psychological mechanisms by associating products with emotional and symbolic meanings rather than purely functional attributes. Research on judgment and decision-making suggests that many everyday decisions are strongly influenced by intuitive and automatic cognitive processes, rather than by deliberate rational analysis.
(Kahneman, 2012)

These insights help explain why advertising appeals that target emotions such as social belonging or fear of missing out can be particularly effective, thereby addressing psychological motives that extend beyond the functional value of consumer goods.



Consequences for Psychological Well-Being

As mentioned, a strong materialistic value orientation has been consistently associated with lower life satisfaction and poorer psychological well-being. Individuals who place high importance on extrinsic goals such as wealth and social status report, on average, higher levels of depressive symptoms, anxiety, and other indicators of psychological distress than those who prioritize intrinsic goals.
(Kasser et al., 2014)

One central explanation for this association lies in the instability of externally defined self-worth. Within the framework of Self-Determination Theory, materialism is understood as a value orientation that emphasizes external rewards and approval. A strong focus on possessions, income, and status may interfere with the satisfaction of basic psychological needs, namely autonomy, competence and relatedness, which in turn increases vulnerability to psychological distress.
(Dittmar et al., 2014)

Materialistic values may also shift attention away from non-material sources of well-being. Empirical research suggests that higher materialism is associated with lower quality of social relationships, reduced engagement in meaningful activities and lower overall need satisfaction. Over time, this imbalance can contribute to experiences of reduced emotional fulfilment and social connectedness, as personal resources are increasingly directed toward consumption rather than personal development or social bonds.
(Kasser et al., 2014)



Conclusion

Overall, psychological research suggests that a strong materialistic value orientation is associated with lower long-term well-being, primarily because it emphasizes external rewards and unstable sources of self-worth. While consumption can provide short-term satisfaction, it often fails to fulfil basic psychological needs, making materialism a fragile and ultimately ineffective pathway to lasting well-being.

Personal Possessions as Carriers of Memory, Identity and Emotional Attachment

Personal possessions function as carriers of memory, identity and emotional attachment in several ways. They act as cues to past experiences and relationships, help anchor and stabilize identity across time and space, and symbolize personal history and self-development. These functions can also support the transmission of memories and values across generations. However, the emotional significance of possessions is shaped by factors such as genders and socioeconomic status, and tends to weaken in contexts of high mobility, where practical use-value often outweighs symbolic meaning.



1 Possessions as carriers of memory

The studies reveal multiple mechanisms through which possessions function as memory carriers. Possessions serve as symbols of interpersonal ties with others and as cues to past experiences. Among functional hoarders, possessions are perceived as threats to memory when considered for disposal, indicating their significant role in memory preservation (Cherrier and Ponnor, 2010).
For older consumers, cherished possessions play a significant role in reminiscence and life review, triggering memories related to life events and personal history. These possessions carry the “storied nature of meanings” that older consumers attach to them, with memory functions bundled with life review and disposition concerns. Possessions are involved in narrative construction or storytelling as they are used to pass on personal and familial legacies (Price et al., 2000).

Scrapbooks represent a specialized form of memory-carrying possession. They function to collect, reconstruct and protect autobiographical memories. The scrapbook preserves photographs and mementos, associating them with narrative and ornamentation. Memory protection strategies employed by scrapbookers include overcollection of memory markers and increased journaling. These modes of memory reconstruction are cued by the scrapbook itself, serving as aide-memoires (Phillips, 2016).
The elderly use possessions for mnemonic purposes, with objects acting as memory cues (Mccracken, 1987).
Possessions are linked to identity and serve as carriers of memory and personal history, storing memories of relationships and personal experiences. Photo-elicitation research demonstrates that possessions trigger memories and are used in narrative construction (Whitmore, 2001).
However, one study presents a contrasting perspective. In the context of global nodanism, possessions do not function as significant carriers of memory. Global nomads do not form strong attachments to possessions and lack of importance placed on curation indicate that possessions do not serve as memory carriers in this mobile context (Bardhi et al., 2012).



2 Possessions as Carriers of Identity

The studies consistently demonstrates that possessions reflect and construct personal identity, though the mechanisms and intensity vary across populations. Possessions reflect personal identity by being considered important for their instrumental, pragmatic and self-referent features for men, while women see them as symbolizing interpersonal integration and emotional attachment. A theoretical continuum from function/self-oriented to symbolic/other-oriented meanings illuminates how possessions reflect gender identity (Dittmar, 1989).
Business commuters view possessions as unique symbols of their personal history and self-development, using possessions to communicate personal history and professional identity (Dittmar, 1991).
Possessions anchor and stabilize identity in space, providing a sense of belonging. During geographical moves, possessions help sustain connections to places and the self, and they manage temporality by connecting past, present, and future selves (Bardhi et al., 2012).

For older consumers, possessions carry symbolic meanings that are part of personal narratives, essential for identity construction. These possessions are used in self-presentation and communication to others through controlled meaning transfer, connecting to personal history and self-development by passing on legacies and achieving symbolic immortality (Price et al., 2000).
Possessions are bundled with personal history and used to transfer this history to younger generations. The act of passing possessions to younger generations serves as a form of self-presentation and communication of identity (Curasi, 2011).
Among the elderly, possessions are critical for self-recognition, reconciliation, and reflection in the last stage of life, assisting in the active construction of identities and embodying individual, social, and existential narratives (Whitmore, 2001).
In global nomadism, however, possessions are less salient as identity carriers and are valued more for instrumental use-value. This represents a “liquid” relationship to possessions characterized by detachment and flexibility, where possessions are valued for their temporary and situational use-value rather than for identity formation or emotional attachment (Bardhi et al., 2012).



3 Possessions as Carriers of Emotional Attachment

Emotional attachment to possessions manifests through multiple dimensions across the studies. Women regard possessions as important because they symbolize interpersonal integration and emotional attachment (Dittmar, 1989).
Among unemployed individuals, possessions demonstrate emotional, mood-adjusting features that provide comfort (Dittmar, 1991).
Functional hoarders perceive possessions as providing security, with throwing away seen as a threat to this security. Possessions serve as symbols of interpersonal ties with others (Cherrier and Ponnor, 2010).
For older consumers, cherished possessions evoke strong and ambivalent emotions. Concerns about disposition involve avoiding intrafamilial conflict and exercising control over the future life of possessions. Intergenerational attachment processes involve passing on personal and familial legacies and achieving symbolic immortality (Price et al., 2000).

Sentimental possessions create reluctance to donate, as consumers experience perceived identity loss when considering donation of sentimental goods. Memory preservation strategies, such as taking photos of possessions before donating, help ameliorate this perceived identity loss (Winterich et al., 2017).
In intergenerational transfers, older consumers are emotionally attached to their possessions and worry about younger family members not caring about these cherished items. Possessions serve to maintain family identity through intergenerational transfer processes. Females demonstrate higher valuing of cherished possessions than males (Curasi, 2011).
The elderly use possessions to negotiate transitions in self and status definition, and possessions serve as instruments of cross-generational influence (Mccracken, 1987).
Among Florida retirees, attachment intensity is high due to possessions’ central role in identity and aging, with emotional significance implying comfort and mood-regulating functions (Whitmore, 2001).
Again, global nomads present an exception, where emotional connections to possessions are not salient, emotional responses to possession loss or transfer are not significant, and attachment intensity is generally lower than in other populations (Bardhi et al., 2012).
